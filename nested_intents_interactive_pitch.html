The computer-age failure mode

Finance became a competition in machine speed.

Electronic markets repeatedly solved execution problems by increasing speed: faster data, faster matching, faster networking, faster cancellation, faster repricing, and faster market makers.

Crypto is replaying TradFi at higher speed

Bitcoin introduced ten-minute block times. Ethereum added programmable execution measured in seconds. Solana pushed public-chain execution toward sub-second latency. Hyperliquid, MegaETH, and other high-performance systems now compete on throughput and latency for increasingly sophisticated strategies.

The infrastructure is impressive, but it risks importing the same market-structure outcome: the largest rewards flow to firms that can afford the fastest systems, privileged infrastructure, and colocated execution.

The goal is not to end automation. The goal is to reduce structural dependence on high-frequency reaction speed as the primary defense against stale execution.

Intent landscape

Existing intents solve outcomes. Nested Intents govern permission.

CoW Swap, NEAR Intents, and Anoma already support declarative requirements. Nested Intents extend that model into persistent programmable execution policy.

NEAR Intents

Anoma

Valid state transitions

Intent-centric applications where solvers discover counterparties and compute valid state transitions.

Ivy

Execution permission

Even if a solver can execute, the user's nested verifier decides whether execution is allowed right now.

System Primary question Nested Intent distinction
CoW-style batch auction Who can settle this batch best? Nested Intents ask whether the proposed execution still satisfies the user's verifier.
NEAR-style intents Who can fulfill this cross-chain outcome best? Nested Intents define when fulfillment is permitted, not only how it is fulfilled.

Core primitive

A base intent plus a persistent execution guard.

The base layer defines what the user wants. The nested layer defines whether execution remains valid whenever a solver attempts to execute.

Base Intent: "What do I want?" Nested Intent: "Under what conditions is execution still valid?" Verifier: canExecute(intent, bid, marketState, oracleState, userLogic) -> true | false

The innovation is not merely a bytes field. The bytes are transport. The primitive is the execution model: a first-class verifier that remains attached to the mandate across its lifetime.

Most expressive form

Nested Intents can transform the underlying fields.

Existing declarative intents check whether fixed conditions are satisfied. Nested Intents can also define how the intent itself should adapt before execution when verified inputs justify the change.

Three possible outcomes

1. canExecute(...) == true -> execute 2. canExecute(...) == false -> block execution 3. transformIntent(...) returns updatedIntent -> modify terms in flight, then verify again

Concrete threshold example

A Nested Intent may initially define a minimum acceptable price of 10 USD, but its nested policy can permit that threshold to adjust to 9.50 USD at execution time if oracle data, on-chain conditions, and user-defined logic justify the change.

transformIntent(intent, marketState, oracleState, userLogic) -> updatedIntent canExecute(updatedIntent, bid, marketState, oracleState) -> true | false
Long-term silent auctions

Execution no longer has to happen instantly.

If the mandate is guarded by a nested verifier, a bid can remain live for hours, weeks, months, or even years without becoming an unsafe stale order.

Without Nested Intents

Market changes force cancel-and-replace cycles. The user protects themselves by updating faster.

With Nested Intents

Market changes are absorbed by the verifier. Execution occurs only when the policy still passes.

BestEx over time

The executor selects the best valid proposal, not simply the fastest or earliest proposal.

Marketplace design

Better exchanges need both sides to trust validity.

Nested Intents let exchanges such as Ivy Markets organize around longer-lived, model-aware liquidity instead of immediate cancellation and replacement.

Sellers get durable indications of interest

Sellers can observe bids that remain governed by explicit validity rules. The bid may adapt as markets move, but it does not become an opaque promise. It remains tied to a verifiable execution policy.

Buyers preserve their risk models

Buyers can submit bids that evolve with market conditions while remaining constrained by their pricing assumptions, exposure limits, liquidity requirements, and model-defined view of fair execution.

Liquidity can remain present during market stress, but executable only when the conditions specified by both buyers and sellers continue to hold.

SYMMIO compute market

Arbitrary verification must be paid for.

If users can attach Turing-complete or arbitrary verification logic, the protocol needs a compute market. Simple checks are cheap; complex models must fund their own evaluation.

ExecutionPriority = ExpectedExecutionProfit + SYMMIOComputeFee - VerificationCost - RiskPenalty

A high-value intent with extremely expensive verification may be less attractive than a slightly lower-value intent with cheap verification, unless the user attaches enough compute budget.

Term Meaning
ComputeRequired The finite work needed to evaluate canExecute(...).
ComputeBudget SYMMIO deposited or authorized by the intent creator.
ComputeFee SYMMIO paid to verifiers, executors, oracle networks, or proof systems.
VerificationCost The actual resource burden of computation, proof, or oracle work.

Simple verifier

Min/max price protection. Low compute. Low fee.

Template verifier

Volatility, liquidity, funding, time window, portfolio exposure, or solver reputation.

Custom verifier

Muon-verified programs, oracle inputs, market statistics, or user-supplied model logic.

Practical V1

Start with min/max price and PartyB passthrough.

The first version does not need a massive custom model. It can start with the simplest valuable nested verifier: execute only if the final execution price is inside the bidder's range.

Frontend states

Future verifier examples

  • Portfolio exposure remains safe
  • Muon oracle confirms custom condition
Trust roadmap

Protocol-owned solver today. Provable BestEx tomorrow.

Ivy can start pragmatically with a protocol-owned solver, then progressively constrain the executor with audit logs, sequencing, TEEs, and ZK proofs.

V0

Protocol-owned solver

The fastest path: a trusted executor monitors intents, sources bids, checks min/max, and executes the best valid bid.

V1

Auditable execution logs

Every execution records intent ID, bid set hash, cutoff, selected bid, rejected bid reasons, verifier result, and settlement calldata hash.

V2

Zellular sequencing

A canonical ordering layer proves which bids existed before cutoff, reducing the executor's ability to hide or rewrite bid history.

V3

TEE attestation

A trusted execution environment attests that the solver ran the correct best-valid-bid algorithm over the sequenced bid set.

V4

ZK proof of best valid bid

A circuit proves the selected bid was eligible, passed the nested verifier, and no better-scoring valid bid existed.

BestValidBid = argmax(score(bid)) subject to: bid arrived before cutoff nestedVerifier(intent, bid, state) == true bid satisfies protocol rules computeBudget >= verificationCost
Final pitch

The market should reward models, not engines.

Nested Intents do not remove solvers, automation, or fast infrastructure. They make those systems accountable to durable user-defined policy.

Before

Orders become stale. Traders update constantly. Speed decides who survives. The market rewards lower latency, faster cancellation, and privileged infrastructure.

After

Mandates remain protected. Verifiers guard execution. Compute is priced explicitly. Pricing models and risk models decide what execution is valid.

We do not beat the speed race by building an even faster engine. We beat it by changing what the market rewards.
